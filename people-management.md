# People Management Coaching Reference

## The CS People Management Reality in the AI Era

Managing a CS team in 2024+ is fundamentally different:
- AI is automating the transactional parts of the CSM role
- The bar for what "good" looks like has risen — CSMs need to be commercial, strategic, and AI-fluent
- Burnout is real: CSMs are being asked to do more with the same or fewer resources
- The CSM role identity is in flux — coach your team through that uncertainty

---

## Hiring

### What to Look For in a CSM Hire (AI Era)

The traditional CSM profile is no longer sufficient. Hire for:

**Must-haves:**
- **Commercial curiosity** — do they think about revenue, not just relationships?
- **Data fluency** — can they read a health score dashboard and form a hypothesis?
- **Communication precision** — clear writer, strong async communicator
- **AI adaptability** — are they excited about or threatened by AI tools?
- **Resilience** — CS is hard. Do they bounce or spiral?

**Red flags in interviews:**
- Talks only about "relationships" with no mention of outcomes or metrics
- Can't articulate how they've influenced a renewal or expansion
- Defensive or dismissive about AI ("I prefer the human touch")
- No curiosity about the business model or how CS fits into company revenue

**Interview questions that reveal the right signals:**

> *"Tell me about a time you saved an account that was about to churn. Walk me through exactly what you did."*
(Look for: specificity, commercial awareness, what they escalated vs. handled alone)

> *"How do you currently use AI in your work? What have you tried?"*
(Look for: genuine curiosity, experimentation, honest about what works)

> *"You have 30 accounts. Three go red at once. How do you prioritize?"*
(Look for: data-driven triage, escalation judgment, knowing when to ask for help)

### Hiring for the AI-Era CSM Role

The job description itself needs updating. Consider adding:
- Proficiency with AI tools (Copilot, ChatGPT, CS platform AI features)
- Ability to interpret predictive churn signals, not just reactive flags
- Experience with scaled/pooled CS models (not just 1:1 high touch)

## Performance Management

### CSM Underperformance: Diagnose Before Acting

**The 3 real causes of CSM underperformance:**
1. **Skill gap** — they don't know how to do something (trainable)
2. **Will gap** — they know but won't do it (manageable differently)
3. **System gap** — the tools, processes, or book of business setup is setting them up to fail (fixable at the manager level)

**Diagnosis questions:**
- Are other CSMs with similar books performing better?
- Do they understand what "good" looks like — explicitly?
- Have they had real feedback, or just hints?
- Is the book of business structured fairly?

**What NOT to do:**
- ❌ Put someone on a PIP when the real issue is a system gap
- ❌ Give vague feedback ("you need to be more proactive")
- ❌ Wait too long — underperformance compounds

### Delivering Difficult Feedback

**The SBI Framework (Situation-Behavior-Impact):**
> *"In last week's QBR with [Customer] [Situation], you spent 40 minutes on product updates and didn't ask about their renewal plans [Behavior]. The customer left without any commitment, and the AE is now asking me what our renewal strategy is [Impact]. I want to understand what happened and how we fix this."*

**Script for a performance conversation:**
> *"I want to be direct with you because I think you can get there, and I'd rather have this conversation now than later. Here's what I'm seeing. Here's what I need to see change. Here's the support I'll give you. And here's the timeline we're working with. What questions do you have?"*

### PIP (Performance Improvement Plan)

**Only go to PIP when:**
- Feedback has been given clearly and repeatedly
- Coaching and support have been offered
- The underperformance is documented
- HR has been consulted

**PIP structure:**
- Specific, measurable outcomes expected (not "improve your communication")
- Clear timeline (typically 30–60 days)
- Weekly check-ins with documented notes
- Named support: manager coaching, training resources
- Explicit statement of consequence if not met

**Script to open a PIP conversation:**
> *"This is a formal performance improvement plan, and I want to be honest with you about what it means. It means I'm concerned about your performance in specific areas, I'm committed to supporting your improvement, and if the outcomes aren't met in [X] weeks, it may result in a change in your role. I want this to work. Let's talk about how."*

## Team Culture & Burnout

### Recognizing Burnout in CS Teams

CS burnout signs:
- CSMs go quiet on Slack/team channels
- Response times to customers slip
- Mistake frequency increases
- Cynicism or negativity in 1:1s ("what's the point")
- Sick days / sudden PTO requests

**The burnout causes specific to CS:**
1. **Account overload** — too many accounts per CSM with no support
2. **Emotional labor tax** — managing angry/demanding customers alone
3. **Lack of wins** — retention is invisible; nothing to celebrate
4. **AI anxiety** — fear of being replaced, unaddressed
5. **Cross-functional friction** — blamed for problems owned by Product or Sales

### Addressing Burnout

**Immediate actions:**
- 1:1 check-in: *"I've noticed [specific observation]. How are you doing? Not with accounts — how are you actually doing?"*
- Temporarily reduce account load if possible
- Celebrate wins publicly — retention is a win, name it

**Structural fixes:**
- Build "CS wins" into team meetings — not just at-risk accounts
- Reduce the number of accounts CSMs carry solo (use pooled model or AI-assisted for low-touch)
- Create a clear escalation path so CSMs aren't isolated with difficult situations

### Building Team Culture

**For a VP CS, culture is a business lever, not a nice-to-have.**

High-performing CS cultures share:
- **Clear commercial accountability** — team knows the NRR number and their role in it
- **Psychological safety to escalate** — CSMs flag risk early, not hide it
- **AI as ally framing** — AI is positioned as removing the admin burden, not replacing the role
- **Career path clarity** — CSMs know how to grow (into leadership, into CS Ops, into Sales)

**Script for team AI anxiety:**
> *"I want to talk about AI directly. The concern about whether AI will replace your role is real and I'm not going to dismiss it. Here's my honest take: the CSMs who will be replaced are the ones who don't adapt. The ones who learn to use AI as a superpower — to carry more accounts, to prep for conversations faster, to spot risk earlier — those are the CSMs companies will fight to keep. That's what we're building toward here."*

## Managing Up & Team Advocacy

### Fighting for Headcount

**Before the ask, answer these questions:**
- What's the current CSM:ARR ratio vs. industry benchmark?
- What's the cost of current churn that additional headcount could prevent?
- What's the specific capacity gap — accounts per CSM, time-to-response, coverage holes?

**The headcount ROI argument:**
> *"Each CSM carries $[X]M ARR. At our current NRR of [X]%, we're losing approximately $[X] annually. One additional CSM could cover [X] accounts with elevated risk. If they save even one of those accounts, the ROI pays for 18 months of fully-loaded cost."*

### 1:1s That Actually Work

**Structure for manager 1:1s with CSMs:**
1. **How they're doing** (not accounts — them personally) — 5 min
2. **Account review** — focus on red/orange accounts, not green — 15 min
3. **Skill development** — one thing you're coaching this week — 10 min
4. **Career conversation** — once a month minimum — 10 min

Don't let 1:1s become account status updates. That's what dashboards are for.
