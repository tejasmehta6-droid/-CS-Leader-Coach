# Career Strategy Coaching Reference

## The CS Leadership Career Landscape (AI Era)

The CS career ladder is being restructured by AI. Here's the honest picture:

**Roles that are growing:**
- VP CS / CCO (strategic, AI-literate leaders who own NRR)
- CS Ops leaders (the people who make AI-powered CS actually work)
- AI-native CSMs (commercial, high-complexity relationship owners)
- CS Enablement (training teams to work with AI)

**Roles under pressure:**
- Transactional CSMs (routine check-ins, low-complexity accounts) — AI is taking over here
- CS Coordinators / Admins — automation is replacing manual work

**The strategic implication for career planning:**
The path forward in CS is toward higher complexity, higher commercial accountability, and AI fluency. Every career move should take you further in at least one of these directions.

## From Director to VP CS

### What Actually Gets You Promoted

**What Directors think gets them promoted:**
- Managing their team well
- Good customer relationships
- Strong NRR in their segment

**What actually gets them promoted:**
1. **Commercial ownership** — owning NRR as a number, not just a metric you track
2. **Executive presence** — showing up in board conversations, CXO discussions with a POV
3. **Organizational influence** — driving cross-functional change (CS + Sales + Product alignment)
4. **Team building** — having promoted people under you, not just retained them
5. **Visibility** — being known outside your immediate team (internal and external personal brand)

### The VP CS Role: What It Actually Is

A VP CS is not a senior Director. It's a fundamentally different job:
- **Director** → executes the CS strategy within the system
- **VP** → designs the CS strategy, the system, and defends it at the board level

The mindset shift: stop thinking about accounts. Start thinking about the machine that handles accounts.

**Questions to pressure-test VP readiness:**
- Can you speak to NRR trend and driver decomposition without looking at a report?
- Have you ever built a CS Ops infrastructure from scratch?
- Have you presented to a board or exec team and influenced a decision?
- Have you hired and developed a manager (not just a CSM)?
- Do you have a clear POV on how AI changes your CS org — in writing?

## Positioning for VP CS Roles (External)

### What Hiring Managers Look For

When SaaS founders, CCOs, and boards hire a VP CS, they want to see:

1. **NRR ownership** — a clear narrative of how you moved NRR, with numbers
2. **Scale** — have you built a team, not just managed one?
3. **Commercial acumen** — do you think like a revenue leader or a service leader?
4. **AI literacy** — are you behind, current, or ahead on AI integration?
5. **Executive communication** — can they put you in a board room without coaching?

### Building Your VP CS Narrative

**The one-paragraph VP CS narrative:**
> *"I've spent [X] years building CS organizations that treat retention as a revenue strategy, not a service function. At [Company], I led a team of [X] CSMs managing $[X]M ARR, and moved NRR from [X]% to [X]% over [X] quarters by [specific mechanism]. I've built the CS Ops infrastructure, designed the team's AI integration roadmap, and own the NRR conversation at the board level. I'm looking for a role where CS is a first-class revenue function."*

**What makes this narrative land:**
- Specific numbers (ARR, NRR before/after, team size)
- Mechanism (not just "I improved NRR" — how?)
- Commercial positioning (revenue, not service)
- AI credibility (can't ignore this in 2024+)

### The Personal Brand Strategy (LinkedIn)

For VP CS positioning, your LinkedIn content should establish you as:
1. **The NRR authority** — you write about retention economics and what actually moves the number
2. **The AI-in-CS voice** — you have a POV on how AI is changing CS leadership (most don't)
3. **The people leadership perspective** — how you build and coach CS teams
4. **The board-level narrator** — how CS leaders communicate upward

**Content cadence for VP CS positioning:**
- 2–3 posts per week
- Mix: 50% insight/data posts, 30% story/lesson posts, 20% opinion/POV posts
- Engage with 5–10 posts from CS leaders, SaaS founders, and CCOs weekly

## From VP CS to CCO

### What Separates CCOs from VP CS Leaders

**CCO competencies that VP CS leaders often lack:**
- **Enterprise-wide revenue ownership** — NRR is table stakes; CCOs own customer lifetime value strategy
- **Product influence** — CCOs drive product roadmap from customer insight
- **M&A and partnership lens** — how does CS scale in an acquisition or partner ecosystem?
- **Organizational design** — CCO builds the CS function across multiple geographies, segments, leaders
- **Investor relations** — CCOs are increasingly expected to present in investor settings

**The CCO positioning shift:**
Stop talking about CS. Start talking about **customer-led growth** as a board-level strategy.

### Building Toward CCO

1. **Own more than CS** — volunteer to take on CS Ops, CS Enablement, or customer marketing
2. **Build board-level relationships** — get in front of board members at your current company
3. **Develop a thought leadership platform** — CCOs are increasingly public-facing voices
4. **Get M&A exposure** — even one acquisition integration gives you rare experience
5. **Build a network of CS peers** — future CCOs know other future CCOs

## Interview Coaching for CS Leaders

### The 5 Questions Every CS Interview Includes

**1. "Walk me through how you've improved NRR."**
Wrong answer: *"We focused on customer success and built strong relationships."*
Right answer: *"We moved NRR from [X]% to [X]% over [X] quarters. The three levers were: [1] early at-risk detection (we implemented predictive scoring that gave us 90 days of warning), [2] structured expansion motion (I built a CQL process with Sales), and [3] EBR redesign (we shifted from backward-looking to forward-looking reviews). The biggest unlock was [specific insight]."*

**2. "How do you think about CS team structure?"**
Show you can design for scale, not just manage an existing structure.
*"It depends on the motion. For enterprise, I think dedicated CSMs per segment with a pooled model for the long tail. For PLG, I'd invert that — CS Ops and tooling do the heavy lifting, humans handle complex and strategic accounts. The key question is: where does human time actually move the NRR needle?"*

**3. "How are you thinking about AI in CS?"**
This is now a mandatory question. Have a real answer.
*"I think about AI at two levels. Tactically: where can it free CSMs from admin to focus on relationships? That's call prep, email drafting, health monitoring. Strategically: how does AI change the number of accounts a CSM can carry effectively, and how does that change our team design and hiring plan? I've [specific thing you've done]."*

**4. "Describe a significant churn situation and how you handled it."**
They want: diagnosis, action, result, lesson.
Be specific, be honest about what went wrong, lead with what you learned.

**5. "How do you manage the CS-Sales relationship?"**
They want to know if you can handle cross-functional friction as a senior leader.
Show that you understand both sides, you've built joint processes, and you think in terms of shared NRR accountability.

### Negotiating the VP CS Offer

**What to negotiate beyond base salary:**
- NRR bonus / revenue retention bonus (align your incentives with the outcome)
- Equity (CS is increasingly seen as a revenue driver — negotiate accordingly)
- Team budget and headcount authority (can you hire without approval for every role?)
- CS Ops resources (do you get dedicated Ops, or are you building tools manually?)
- Title precision: VP CS vs. SVP CS vs. CCO matters for your next move

**The negotiation frame:**
> *"I'm excited about this role and want to make it work. The one thing I want to make sure we align on is the NRR target and what resources I'll have to hit it. If I'm owning [X]% NRR, I want to make sure we agree on [headcount, tools, budget] so we're both set up for success."*
