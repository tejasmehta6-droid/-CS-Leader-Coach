# Executive & Board Stakeholder Coaching Reference

## The Core Insight

Most CS leaders are excellent customer advocates. The best CS leaders are also excellent executives.
The gap: **knowing how to translate customer reality into board-level language.**

CS leaders who don't develop this skill stay Directors forever.
CS leaders who do become VPs, CCOs, and board advisors.

---

## Internal Executive Stakeholders

### Managing Your CEO / CCO / CRO

**What they actually care about:**
- NRR trajectory (not individual accounts)
- Risk concentration (what % of ARR is at risk)
- Whether CS is a cost center or a growth engine
- Whether you're ahead of problems or reacting to them

**How to show up as a strategic CS leader in exec conversations:**

1. **Lead with the number, then the story.** Don't walk them through the narrative to arrive at the number. Start with: *"NRR is at 104% this quarter, up from 101% last quarter. Here's what's driving it and what I'm watching."*

2. **Bring a POV, not just a report.** Executives don't want a data dump. They want: here's what I see, here's what it means, here's what I'm doing about it.

3. **Quantify risk explicitly.** *"We have $[X]M ARR in orange/red status. Of that, $[X]M is saveable with the right intervention in the next 30 days. Here's the plan."*

4. **Own bad news early.** The worst thing a CS leader can do is let executives be surprised by churn. Flag risk early, even when you don't have the full save plan yet.

**Script for a proactive risk conversation with your CEO:**
> *"I want to flag something before it becomes a surprise. [Customer] is showing signals that their renewal is at risk — [specific signals]. I don't think it's gone yet, and here's what I'm doing to save it. I wanted you to know so you're not caught off guard and in case there's an exec relationship lever we should pull."*

### CS vs. Sales Alignment

This is the most common internal friction point for CS leaders.

**The core tensions:**
- Sales overpromises → CS inherits impossible expectations
- Sales wants to expand accounts CS is still onboarding
- CS flags at-risk → Sales argues CS is being too conservative
- Commission structures misalign incentives

**How to frame the CS-Sales relationship:**

Not: CS cleans up Sales's mess.
Not: Sales closes, CS retains.
Yes: **CS and Sales are joint owners of revenue growth from existing customers.** CS protects the base. Sales expands it. Both win on NRR.

**Script for a CS-Sales alignment conversation:**
> *"I want to find a model where we're not fighting over the same accounts — we're driving them together. Can we agree on: here's when CS leads, here's when Sales leads, and here's how we hand off expansion signals? I think if we get this right, both teams' numbers improve."*

**What to escalate when Sales-CS friction is structural:**
If misalignment is systematic (not just one AE), escalate to the CRO with data:
- Accounts where overselling led to churn (with ARR figures)
- Proposed solution (not just the complaint)
- What you need: joint OKRs, shared NRR accountability, cleaner handoff process

## Board-Level Stakeholder Management

### What CS Leaders Get Wrong in Board Conversations

- They present account stories when the board wants revenue trends
- They use CS jargon (health scores, QBR) without defining commercial impact
- They're defensive about churn instead of analytical
- They present NRR as a snapshot instead of a trend with leading indicators

### The Board CS Narrative Framework

Structure every board CS update with:

**1. The headline metric (30 seconds)**
NRR, GRR, logo retention rate — trend, not snapshot.
*"NRR is 106%, up from 102% last quarter and 98% a year ago. We've now had three consecutive quarters of NRR improvement."*

**2. What's driving the number (2 minutes)**
Decompose: expansion contribution, churn drivers, segment performance.
*"Expansion revenue from existing customers grew 18% QoQ, driven primarily by [segment]. Logo churn was 3% — consistent with last quarter. Revenue churn was lower at 1.8% because the logos we lost were smaller accounts."*

**3. Risk landscape (1 minute)**
Be explicit about concentration risk.
*"We currently have $[X]M ARR in elevated risk status. The top 3 at-risk accounts represent [X]% of that. Here's our intervention plan and confidence level for each."*

**4. CS as a growth lever (1 minute)**
Position CS proactively as a growth engine, not a retention backstop.
*"CS-sourced expansion (CQLs passed to Sales) generated $[X]M in new ARR this quarter. Our goal is to grow this contribution by [X]% next quarter by [specific mechanism]."*

**5. The AI / efficiency angle (30 seconds — increasingly expected)**
*"We're in [phase] of our AI integration plan. Current impact: [X]% reduction in CSM admin time, [X] accounts per CSM target being reached. Next milestone: predictive churn signals live in Q[X]."*

### Talking to Investors / Board About Churn

**Don't:**
- Be vague about causes (*"macroeconomic headwinds"* without specifics)
- Present NRR without trend context
- Treat a churn spike as a one-off without structural analysis

**Do:**
- Decompose churn: avoidable vs. structural, large vs. small accounts
- Show you understand the root cause
- Present the systemic fix, not just the individual account save

**Script for addressing a bad NRR quarter:**
> *"NRR came in at 94% this quarter, below our target of 100%. I want to walk you through what happened and what we're doing. Of the $[X]M churned, $[X]M was from [segment] accounts where [root cause]. We've already [specific action taken]. The remaining $[X]M was [cause] — this is the structural issue we're addressing by [fix]. I expect this to stabilize in Q[X+1] and return to target by Q[X+2]. Here's what I'll be watching."*

## Customer Executive Relationship Management

### Building Executive Relationships Before You Need Them

Most CS teams only activate exec-to-exec relationships when an account is at risk. That's too late.

**Build the relationship when the account is healthy:**
- Quarterly exec sponsor touchpoints (not QBRs — relationship calls)
- Pull your CCO or VP CS into strategic accounts annually
- Celebrate wins at the exec level — *"Your team achieved [outcome], I wanted your CRO to know."*

### EBR (Executive Business Review) Design

An EBR that actually works:
1. **Opens with their business goals** — not yours, not your product's features
2. **Presents outcomes achieved** — in their language, their metrics
3. **Surfaces challenges honestly** — don't hide what's not working
4. **Looks forward** — next 12 months, what success looks like together
5. **Closes with a commitment** — both sides leave with something to do

**What NOT to put in an EBR:**
- ❌ Product roadmap (this is a Sales / product conversation, not an EBR)
- ❌ Your company's achievements (they don't care)
- ❌ Health scores (meaningless to customers)
- ❌ A slide deck that could apply to any customer

**EBR opening script:**
> *"Before we get into our presentation, I want to make sure we're focused on what matters most to you right now. What are the top 1–2 things on your mind for [this year / next quarter]?"*
Then listen. Adjust the rest of the EBR to what they said.

### Difficult Customer Conversations

**When a customer is angry:**
1. Don't defend. Don't explain. Absorb first.
2. Acknowledge specifically: *"You're right that [X] didn't happen the way it should have."*
3. Then: *"Here's what I'm committing to."* (Be specific. Timeline. Owner.)
4. Follow up faster than promised.

**When a customer escalates to your CEO:**
Don't be blindsided. Brief your CEO before the call:
> *"[Customer] is escalating because [issue]. Here's the context. Here's what CS has done. Here's what I recommend you say. The goal of the call is [outcome]."*
